ADGI Week 2025

An identity representing design as process, not product

ADGI Design Week is a national-scale exhibition initiated by the Asosiasi Desainer Grafis Indonesia (Indonesian Graphic Designers Association), positioning Indonesian graphic design within a global context. The 2025 theme, Poliformat, frames design as a process that moves across mediums, disciplines, and generations.

This project takes that context and responds by treating the visual identity not as a fixed outcome, but as a mindset. The system prioritizes continuity over consistency and adaptability over finality, allowing the identity to shift across formats, information densities, and moments while remaining cohesive throughout the week.

Approach & Role

Event Identity | Visual System | Digital + Print Rollout

I was responsible for translating the Poliformat theme into a visual approach and developing the core logic of the identity. The focus was on designing relationships between typography, layout, and content rather than isolated compositions. The system was tested across multiple applications with varying information density, requiring the identity to remain legible and recognizable while adapting to different formats. This process emphasized thinking in terms of rules and principles, rather than relying on decorative or fixed solutions.

Challenges & Learning

A key challenge was letting go of overly controlled layouts. Early explorations prioritized visual impact but quickly failed when applied to real content and repeated use. This exposed the limits of designing outcomes instead of systems. Through iteration, I learned that flexibility comes from restraint and clarity, not added complexity. Designing for change required refining constraints, accepting variation, and trusting the system to carry the identity over time rather than forcing consistency in every application.

The goal wasn’t to make something visually pleasing. It was to create an identity that communicates a way of thinking, one that reflects design as an active process rather than a finished object.

Built for real conditions, the system responds to change, repetition, and constraint. Its structure encourages viewers to notice continuity, variation, and rhythm over time, inviting them to engage with the work as something unfolding rather than static.

If this project feels like it’s moving even when it’s still, that’s the point.
